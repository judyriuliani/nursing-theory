From Problem to Theory

Choosing a nursing theory
for a problem you care about.

Theory follows problem. This guide walks you from a practice problem, through the kind of knowledge it needs, to the grand and middle-range theories whose assumptions are likely to fit.

01 / Begin with the problem

What practice problem do you care about?

Name it concretely. A patient population, a clinical pattern, a recurring frustration. The problem comes first; the theory comes in service of the problem.

What kind of problem is this?

03 / Patterns of knowing

What kinds of knowledge does the problem need?

Most problems need more than one; pick the one or two that feel most central. A symptom management problem leans on empirics; a communication problem on esthetics and personal knowing; an end-of-life problem almost always requires ethics.

Suggested from your problem and framing
Select up to two. Don't overthink it; the goal is a center of gravity, not a complete map.
04 / Your view of nursing's metaparadigm

What do you believe about person, environment, health, and nursing?

A theory whose assumptions clash with your own beliefs will frustrate you in practice. For each concept, pick the statement closest to your view.

A person is fundamentally…
Different theories assume different ontologies. Roy's biopsychosocial system and Rogers' irreducible unitary being lead to very different practice.
The environment matters most as…
Stressors to defend against, a cultural world to be congruent with, a political/organizational structure, or a caring milieu?
Health is best understood as…
Theories diverge sharply here. Adaptation, comfort, wholeness, transcendence: these are different ends.
Nursing, at its core, is…
A science, an art, a system intervention, or moral-political advocacy? Most theories blend these but lead with one.
05 / Theories worth considering

Theories that fit your problem, ranked by alignment.

Top matches first. Each card explains why the theory fits, and where it might pull you. Multiple theories may work; read the top two or three side by side before committing.

A reminder. This tool is a thinking aid, not a verdict. The patterns of knowing are heuristic; most clinical problems need more than one. And theory fit is iterative: try framing your problem with your top match, then with your second. The friction between them is often where the real intellectual work happens.
